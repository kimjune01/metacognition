# Plate Tectonics and the History of Navigation

## Part I: Plate Tectonics

Plate tectonics is the scientific theory that Earth's lithosphere comprises a number of large tectonic plates, which have been slowly moving since about 3.4 billion years ago. The model builds on the concept of continental drift, an idea developed during the first decades of the 20th century. Plate tectonics came to be accepted by the geoscientific community after the concepts of seafloor spreading were later developed in the late 1950s and early 1960s.

Earth's lithosphere, which is the rigid outermost shell of a planet, is broken into seven or eight major plates, many minor plates, and even smaller micro plates. Where the plates meet, their relative motion determines the type of plate boundary: convergent, divergent, or transform. Earthquakes, volcanic activity, mountain-building, and oceanic trench formation occur along these plate boundaries. The relative movement of the plates typically ranges from zero to 100 mm annually.

Tectonic plates are composed of the oceanic lithosphere and the thicker continental lithosphere, each topped by its own kind of crust. Along convergent plate boundaries, the process of subduction carries the edge of one plate down under the other plate and into the mantle. This process reduces the total surface area of the crust. The creation of new oceanic crust at mid-ocean ridges is linked to this process. The production of new seafloor and oceanic lithosphere occurs through a process called seafloor spreading at mid-ocean ridges. In this way, the total surface of the lithosphere remains the same.

The driving mechanism of plate tectonics is a subject of continuing research. Possible driving forces include mantle convection and slab pull. Mantle convection is the slow, creeping motion of Earth's solid silicate mantle caused by convection currents carrying heat from the interior to the planet's surface. As tectonic plates move on the surface, the ocean floor is subducted under the leading edges of the plates at convergent boundaries. At the same time, the upwelling of mantle material at divergent boundaries creates mid-ocean ridges. The combination of these processes recycles the oceanic lithosphere back into the mantle.

### Historical Development

The theory of plate tectonics was proposed in 1912 by Alfred Wegener as a theory of continental drift. The scientific community rejected his theory partly because he could not provide a convincing mechanism for continental motion. In 1926, Arthur Holmes suggested that thermal convection in the mantle was the driving force. In 1962, geologist Harry Hess proposed the theory of seafloor spreading, which explained how new oceanic crust forms at mid-ocean ridges and then moves away from the ridge. By 1966, most scientists were convinced by the evidence, and plate tectonics became the dominant paradigm.

The distribution of fossils across continents is one line of evidence for plate tectonics. Mesosaurus, a freshwater reptile from the early Permian period, has been found in both southern Africa and eastern South America. Lystrosaurus, a dicynodont therapsid, had a wide range across Pangaea and its fossils are found in Antarctica, India, and South Africa. Glossopteris, a seed fern, is found across all southern continents, suggesting they were once joined.

Paleomagnetic evidence also supports the theory. Rocks of different ages show different apparent polar wander paths. When the continents are reassembled into Pangaea, these paths converge, confirming that the continents have moved. The magnetic striping of the ocean floor, where bands of normal and reversed polarity alternate symmetrically about mid-ocean ridges, provided a key piece of evidence for seafloor spreading.

### The Pacific Ring of Fire

The Pacific Ring of Fire, a major area in the basin of the Pacific Ocean where many earthquakes and volcanic eruptions occur, is a direct result of plate tectonics. It is home to about 75 percent of the world's active and dormant volcanoes. About 90 percent of the world's earthquakes and about 81 percent of the world's largest earthquakes occur along the Ring of Fire.

The Ring of Fire stretches for approximately 40,000 kilometers along the Pacific coast. It runs from New Zealand, along the eastern edge of Asia, north across the Aleutian Islands of Alaska, and south along the western coast of North and South America. The ring includes 452 volcanoes, including Mount Fuji in Japan, Mount St. Helens in the United States, and Mount Pinatubo in the Philippines.

The concept of plate tectonics transformed geological understanding. Before it, the Earth sciences lacked a unifying theory that could explain the distribution of mountains, earthquakes, and volcanoes. The theory explains why earthquakes and volcanoes concentrate along narrow belts, why mountain ranges are found at certain locations, and why the rocks on the ocean floor are younger than those on land.

### Modern Measurements

Modern geodetic techniques, including satellite laser ranging and the Global Positioning System, directly measure the motion of tectonic plates. These measurements confirm that plates move at rates of a few centimeters per year. The North American Plate, for instance, moves westward at about 2.3 centimeters per year relative to the Eurasian Plate.

The Indian Plate moves northward at about 5 centimeters per year, colliding with the Eurasian Plate to form the Himalayas. This collision began approximately 50 million years ago and continues today. The Himalayas are still rising at a rate of about 5 millimeters per year. Mount Everest, the highest point on Earth at 8,849 meters, owes its existence to this ongoing plate collision.

The Mid-Atlantic Ridge is a divergent plate boundary that runs the length of the Atlantic Ocean from the Arctic Ocean to near Antarctica. The ridge sits atop a broad rise that extends to about 1,000 kilometers on either side. At the ridge, new oceanic crust is created as magma rises from below. The rate of spreading varies from about 2.5 centimeters per year in the North Atlantic to about 3.5 centimeters per year in the South Atlantic.

### Subduction Zones

Subduction zones are among the most geologically active areas on Earth. When oceanic lithosphere collides with another plate, one plate slides beneath the other into the mantle, creating a subduction zone. The descending plate heats up as it sinks deeper into the mantle. At depths of about 100 to 200 kilometers, the plate releases water and other volatiles, which lower the melting point of the overlying mantle rock. This generates magma, which rises to the surface to create volcanic arcs.

The Mariana Trench, the deepest part of the world's oceans, was formed by subduction of the Pacific Plate beneath the Mariana Plate. It reaches a maximum depth of nearly 11,000 meters below sea level. The pressure at the bottom of the trench is over 1,000 times atmospheric pressure at sea level.

The Cascadia Subduction Zone extends approximately 1,000 kilometers from northern California to southern British Columbia. It is capable of producing earthquakes of magnitude 9.0 or greater. The last such earthquake occurred in 1700 and generated a tsunami that crossed the Pacific Ocean. Scientists estimate a roughly 37 percent probability of a magnitude 7.1 or greater earthquake in the Cascadia Subduction Zone within the next 50 years.

### Transform Boundaries

Transform boundaries occur where plates slide horizontally past each other. The most famous transform boundary is the San Andreas Fault in California, which extends roughly 1,200 kilometers. The Pacific Plate moves northwestward relative to the North American Plate at about 46 millimeters per year. This motion has been occurring for about 28 million years.

The Dead Sea Transform is another significant transform boundary. It connects the East African Rift in the south with the collision zone in southern Turkey to the north. The Dead Sea, situated along this transform, lies at the lowest point on Earth's surface at about 430 meters below sea level.

Transform faults also occur along mid-ocean ridges, offsetting the ridge segments. These oceanic transform faults can be hundreds of kilometers long. The Romanche Fracture Zone in the equatorial Atlantic is over 900 kilometers long and offsets the Mid-Atlantic Ridge by about 880 kilometers.

## Part II: History of Navigation

Navigation is the process of monitoring and controlling the movement of a craft or vehicle from one place to another. The field encompasses several major categories: marine navigation, aeronautic navigation, and land navigation.

### Ancient Navigation

The earliest navigators relied on landmarks, the sun, and the stars to determine their position and course. Polynesian navigators crossed thousands of miles of open ocean using observations of star positions, wave patterns, and the flight paths of birds. They developed sophisticated mental maps of the Pacific Ocean that allowed them to navigate between islands separated by vast distances.

The Phoenicians were among the first Mediterranean peoples to venture into the open ocean. By about 600 BCE, they had circumnavigated Africa. They used the North Star for navigation at night and the sun's position during the day. Their navigational skills enabled them to establish trading posts throughout the Mediterranean and along the Atlantic coast of Europe and Africa.

Ancient Greek navigators contributed significantly to the science of navigation. Pytheas of Massalia sailed to Britain and possibly Iceland around 325 BCE. He was the first to describe the midnight sun and to connect the tides with the moon. Eratosthenes calculated the circumference of the Earth with remarkable accuracy around 240 BCE, a measurement that was useful for long-distance navigation.

### The Magnetic Compass

The magnetic compass was one of the most important inventions in the history of navigation. The Chinese were the first to discover that a magnetized needle would point north. The earliest reference to a magnetic compass used for navigation dates to 1088 CE in China. The compass reached Europe by the 12th century, possibly through Arab traders.

Before the compass, European sailors relied on coastal landmarks and celestial navigation. Open ocean voyages were extremely dangerous and usually only undertaken during clear weather. The compass allowed navigators to determine direction regardless of weather conditions, enabling voyages across open water even in overcast skies.

The compass did not immediately replace older methods of navigation. For centuries, navigators used a combination of compass readings, dead reckoning, and celestial observations. The compass rose, which divides the circle of the horizon into 32 points, became a standard feature on nautical charts by the 15th century.

### Celestial Navigation

Celestial navigation uses the positions of celestial bodies to determine one's position on Earth. The basic principle involves measuring the angle between a celestial body and the horizon using an instrument such as a sextant. This measurement, combined with the exact time and astronomical tables, allows the navigator to calculate a line of position.

The astrolabe, an ancient instrument for measuring the altitude of celestial bodies, was widely used by Arab and European navigators from the 8th century onward. The cross-staff, introduced in the 14th century, was simpler to use at sea. The backstaff, invented by John Davis in 1594, allowed the navigator to take solar observations with their back to the sun, avoiding the problem of looking directly at the sun.

The sextant, invented in the 18th century, became the standard instrument for celestial navigation. It uses a system of mirrors to bring the image of a celestial body down to the horizon, allowing precise measurement of the angle. A skilled navigator can determine position to within about one nautical mile using a sextant.

### The Longitude Problem

Determining latitude at sea was relatively straightforward using celestial observations. Longitude, however, proved far more difficult. The problem of longitude was one of the greatest scientific challenges of the 17th and 18th centuries. Without an accurate method for determining longitude, ships frequently became lost or ran aground.

In 1714, the British Parliament established the Board of Longitude and offered a prize of 20,000 pounds for a practical method of determining longitude at sea to within half a degree. The astronomical method, championed by the Astronomer Royal Nevil Maskelyne, involved measuring the angular distance between the moon and certain stars. This method was technically sound but required extensive calculations.

John Harrison, a self-taught clockmaker, pursued a different approach. He reasoned that if a navigator could carry an accurate clock set to the time at a reference meridian, comparing local time with the clock time would give the longitude. Harrison spent decades perfecting his marine chronometers. His H4, completed in 1761, was the size of a large pocket watch and kept time to within a few seconds over months at sea.

### Charts and Cartography

Nautical charts are essential tools for marine navigation. The earliest known nautical charts are the portolan charts of the 13th century Mediterranean. These charts featured networks of rhumb lines radiating from compass roses, allowing navigators to plot courses between ports.

Gerardus Mercator published his famous world map in 1569 using a projection that became standard for nautical charts. The Mercator projection represents lines of constant compass bearing as straight lines on the chart, making it invaluable for navigation. However, it distorts the size of land masses near the poles, making Greenland appear similar in size to Africa when Africa is actually 14 times larger.

The British Admiralty began systematically producing nautical charts in 1795 under the direction of Alexander Dalrymple, the first Hydrographer of the Navy. Today, the United Kingdom Hydrographic Office produces about 3,300 nautical charts covering most of the world's navigable waters. Similar organizations exist in other maritime nations.

### Modern Electronic Navigation

The development of radio in the early 20th century revolutionized navigation. Radio direction finding allowed navigators to determine the bearing to a radio transmitter, providing a line of position. By taking bearings on two or more transmitters, a navigator could determine position.

The LORAN (Long Range Navigation) system, developed during World War II, used pairs of radio transmitters to provide position fixes over large areas of the ocean. LORAN-C, the improved version, provided accuracy to within about 460 meters. The system was widely used by both military and civilian navigators until it was replaced by satellite navigation.

The Transit satellite navigation system, operational from 1964, was the first satellite-based navigation system. It used the Doppler shift of radio signals from orbiting satellites to determine position. Transit could provide accuracy to within about 200 meters but required several minutes to obtain a position fix.

The Global Positioning System represents the current state of the art in navigation technology. Developed by the United States Department of Defense, GPS uses a constellation of at least 24 satellites orbiting at about 20,200 kilometers altitude. Each satellite transmits a precise timing signal. A GPS receiver calculates its distance from multiple satellites by measuring the time delay of the received signals. With signals from four or more satellites, the receiver can determine its three-dimensional position to within a few meters.

The Russian GLONASS system provides similar capability. The European Galileo system began full operational service in recent years. China's BeiDou system achieved global coverage as well. These systems, collectively known as Global Navigation Satellite Systems, provide redundancy and improved accuracy when used together.

### Inertial Navigation

Inertial navigation systems use accelerometers and gyroscopes to continuously calculate position, velocity, and orientation without external references. The basic principle involves measuring accelerations and integrating them over time to determine velocity and position changes from a known starting point.

The first practical inertial navigation system was developed for the V-2 rocket during World War II. After the war, the technology was refined for use in aircraft, submarines, and spacecraft. The USS Nautilus used an inertial navigation system during its historic voyage under the North Pole in 1958.

Modern inertial navigation systems use ring laser gyroscopes or fiber optic gyroscopes instead of mechanical gyroscopes. These solid-state devices have no moving parts and are more reliable and accurate than their mechanical predecessors. However, all inertial navigation systems suffer from drift, a gradual accumulation of errors over time. For this reason, inertial systems are typically used in combination with GPS or other external references.

### Navigation in the Air

The development of aviation created new navigational challenges. Early aviators navigated by visual reference to the ground, following roads, rivers, and railroad tracks. This method, called pilotage, was limited by weather conditions and darkness.

The development of instrument flight rules and radio navigation aids in the 1930s and 1940s allowed aircraft to fly in poor visibility. The VOR (VHF Omnidirectional Range) system, introduced in the late 1940s, provides bearing information to aircraft. DME (Distance Measuring Equipment) provides distance information. Together, VOR and DME allow pilots to determine their position relative to ground stations.

The instrument landing system guides aircraft during approach and landing in poor visibility. It uses two radio beams to define the correct approach path in both the horizontal and vertical planes. Category III ILS systems can guide aircraft to landing in near-zero visibility conditions.

Area navigation systems, including GPS, have largely replaced ground-based radio navigation for en route flying. Modern airliners use flight management systems that integrate multiple navigation sources, including GPS, inertial navigation, and radio navigation, to provide precise position information throughout all phases of flight.

### The Future of Navigation

Autonomous vehicles represent the next frontier in navigation technology. Self-driving cars use a combination of GPS, lidar, cameras, and radar to perceive their environment and navigate through traffic. Maritime autonomous surface ships are being developed for cargo transport. Unmanned aerial vehicles rely on GPS and inertial navigation for both military and civilian applications.

Quantum navigation is an emerging field that promises extremely precise measurements without reliance on external signals. Quantum sensors based on atom interferometry can measure acceleration and rotation with extraordinary precision. Such systems could provide navigation capability that is immune to jamming or spoofing of satellite signals.

Indoor navigation remains a significant challenge. GPS signals are generally too weak to penetrate buildings. Alternative approaches include Wi-Fi fingerprinting, Bluetooth beacons, magnetic field mapping, and visual positioning systems. These technologies are increasingly being integrated into smartphones and other devices to provide navigation within airports, shopping malls, and other large indoor spaces.

## Part III: Oceanography

Oceanography is the scientific study of the ocean. It encompasses a wide range of topics, including marine organisms and ecosystem dynamics, ocean currents, waves and geophysical fluid dynamics, plate tectonics and the geology of the sea floor, and fluxes of various chemical substances and physical properties within the ocean and across its boundaries. These diverse topics reflect multiple disciplines that oceanographers blend to further knowledge of the world ocean and understanding of processes within it.

### Physical Oceanography

Physical oceanography studies the physical conditions and processes within the ocean. It encompasses ocean currents, waves, tides, and the distribution of temperature and salinity. The ocean is divided into several major basins: the Pacific, Atlantic, Indian, Southern, and Arctic Oceans. Together they cover approximately 361 million square kilometers, about 71 percent of the Earth's surface.

Ocean currents are continuous, directed movements of ocean water. Surface currents are primarily driven by wind, while deep ocean currents are driven by differences in water density caused by variations in temperature and salinity, a process known as thermohaline circulation. The Gulf Stream, one of the strongest ocean currents, carries warm water from the Gulf of Mexico northeastward across the Atlantic Ocean. It transports about 30 million cubic meters of water per second, more than all the rivers in the world combined.

The thermohaline circulation, sometimes called the ocean conveyor belt, is a global system of deep ocean currents that plays a crucial role in regulating Earth's climate. Cold, dense water sinks in the North Atlantic near Greenland and Iceland, flows southward along the ocean floor, circulates through the Southern Ocean, and eventually rises to the surface in the Pacific and Indian Oceans. A complete circuit of this system takes approximately 1,000 years.

Ocean waves are generated primarily by wind blowing across the water surface. The size of waves depends on three factors: wind speed, the duration of time the wind blows, and the distance of open water over which the wind blows, known as the fetch. The largest wind-generated waves can reach heights of over 30 meters in extreme storms.

Tides are the rise and fall of sea levels caused by the combined gravitational effects of the Moon and the Sun. The Moon's gravitational pull is about twice as effective as the Sun's in producing tides because, although the Sun is much more massive, the Moon is much closer to Earth. Spring tides occur when the Sun, Moon, and Earth are aligned, producing unusually high and low tides. Neap tides occur when the Sun and Moon are at right angles relative to Earth, producing moderate tides.

### Chemical Oceanography

Chemical oceanography studies the chemical composition and chemical processes of the ocean. Seawater contains dissolved salts, gases, organic substances, and undissolved particles. The average salinity of seawater is about 35 parts per thousand, meaning that about 3.5 percent of the weight of seawater comes from dissolved salts. Sodium chloride accounts for about 85 percent of the dissolved salts.

The ocean plays a critical role in the global carbon cycle. It absorbs about 25 percent of the carbon dioxide released by human activities each year. This absorption helps moderate the rate of climate change but also causes ocean acidification. The pH of surface ocean water has decreased by about 0.1 units since the beginning of the Industrial Revolution, representing a roughly 30 percent increase in acidity.

Dissolved oxygen in the ocean is essential for marine life. Oxygen enters the ocean through air-sea gas exchange at the surface and through photosynthesis by marine plants and phytoplankton. Oxygen concentrations are highest near the surface and decrease with depth to a minimum zone at about 200 to 1,000 meters, where biological oxygen demand exceeds supply.

### Biological Oceanography

Biological oceanography studies the organisms that inhabit the ocean and their interactions with the marine environment. The ocean contains an estimated 230,000 known species, but the total number may be over two million. Marine organisms range from microscopic bacteria and phytoplankton to the blue whale, the largest animal ever to have lived on Earth.

Phytoplankton are microscopic photosynthetic organisms that form the base of most marine food webs. They produce approximately half of the world's oxygen through photosynthesis. Phytoplankton blooms, which occur when conditions are favorable for rapid growth, can be large enough to be visible from space. These blooms play an important role in the carbon cycle by absorbing carbon dioxide from the atmosphere.

Coral reefs are among the most diverse ecosystems on Earth. Although they cover less than one percent of the ocean floor, they support approximately 25 percent of all marine species. The Great Barrier Reef, off the coast of Australia, is the world's largest coral reef system, stretching over 2,300 kilometers. Coral reefs face significant threats from ocean warming, acidification, pollution, and overfishing.

The deep ocean, below about 200 meters, was long thought to be a lifeless desert. However, exploration has revealed diverse communities of organisms adapted to extreme conditions. Hydrothermal vents, first discovered in 1977 near the Galapagos Islands, support ecosystems based on chemosynthesis rather than photosynthesis. Organisms at these vents derive energy from chemical reactions involving hydrogen sulfide and other compounds released from the Earth's interior.

### Marine Geology

Marine geology studies the structure and composition of the ocean floor. The ocean floor is divided into several major features: continental shelves, continental slopes, abyssal plains, mid-ocean ridges, and deep-sea trenches.

Continental shelves are the submerged extensions of continents, extending from the shoreline to a depth of about 200 meters. They vary in width from a few kilometers to over 1,000 kilometers. Continental shelves are geologically part of the continent and are composed of continental crust. They are economically important as sites for fishing, oil and gas extraction, and mineral mining.

Abyssal plains are flat areas of the ocean floor at depths of 4,000 to 6,000 meters. They cover approximately 40 percent of the ocean floor and are among the flattest features on Earth. The sediment on abyssal plains consists of fine particles of clay and the remains of microscopic organisms that slowly settle from the water above. Sedimentation rates on abyssal plains are typically only a few centimeters per thousand years.

Mid-ocean ridges are underwater mountain ranges that form along divergent plate boundaries. The global mid-ocean ridge system extends for over 65,000 kilometers and is the longest mountain range on Earth. At the crests of mid-ocean ridges, new oceanic crust is formed as magma rises from the mantle and solidifies. The rocks at the ridge crest are the youngest on the ocean floor, becoming progressively older with distance from the ridge.

## Part IV: Volcanic Activity

Volcanic activity is the eruption of molten rock, known as magma, from beneath the Earth's surface. When magma reaches the surface, it is called lava. Volcanic eruptions can be explosive, producing ash clouds and pyroclastic flows, or effusive, producing slow-moving lava flows. The type of eruption depends on the composition of the magma, particularly its silica content and dissolved gas content.

### Types of Volcanoes

Shield volcanoes are broad, gently sloping volcanoes built by the eruption of fluid basaltic lava. Mauna Loa in Hawaii is the world's largest active shield volcano, rising about 4,170 meters above sea level and extending about 5,000 meters below sea level to the ocean floor. Including the portion below sea level, it is one of the tallest mountains on Earth measured from base to summit.

Stratovolcanoes, also called composite volcanoes, are steep-sided volcanoes built of alternating layers of lava flows, volcanic ash, and other volcanic debris. They tend to produce more explosive eruptions than shield volcanoes. Mount Vesuvius, which destroyed the Roman cities of Pompeii and Herculaneum in 79 CE, is a stratovolcano. Mount Rainier in Washington State, at 4,392 meters, is the tallest stratovolcano in the Cascade Range.

Cinder cones are the simplest type of volcano. They are built from particles and blobs of congealed lava ejected from a single vent. As the gas-charged lava is blown into the air, it breaks into small fragments that solidify and fall as cinders around the vent to form a circular or oval cone. Most cinder cones are relatively small, rarely exceeding 300 meters in height.

Calderas are large, basin-shaped depressions formed when a volcano collapses into the empty magma chamber beneath it. The Yellowstone Caldera in Wyoming is one of the world's largest active calderas, measuring about 55 by 72 kilometers. The most recent caldera-forming eruption at Yellowstone occurred about 640,000 years ago and ejected approximately 1,000 cubic kilometers of volcanic material.

### Notable Eruptions

The eruption of Mount Tambora in Indonesia in 1815 was the most powerful volcanic eruption in recorded history. It ejected approximately 160 cubic kilometers of material and killed an estimated 71,000 people directly. The volcanic aerosols injected into the stratosphere caused global cooling, leading to 1816 being known as the Year Without a Summer. Crops failed across Europe and North America, leading to widespread famine.

The eruption of Krakatoa in 1883 produced one of the loudest sounds in recorded history, heard over 4,800 kilometers away. The eruption generated tsunamis up to 30 meters high that killed over 36,000 people. Fine volcanic ash circled the globe, producing vivid red sunsets for months afterward. The eruption ejected about 25 cubic kilometers of rock and ash.

Mount Pinatubo in the Philippines erupted in 1991, ejecting about 10 cubic kilometers of material. It was the second-largest volcanic eruption of the 20th century. The eruption injected about 20 million tons of sulfur dioxide into the stratosphere, causing global temperatures to drop by about 0.5 degrees Celsius over the following year. The eruption also destroyed Clark Air Base, a major American military installation.

### Volcanic Hazards

Pyroclastic flows are fast-moving currents of hot gas and volcanic matter that travel along the ground away from a volcano at speeds of up to 700 kilometers per hour and at temperatures of up to 1,000 degrees Celsius. They are the most dangerous of all volcanic phenomena. The destruction of Pompeii in 79 CE was caused primarily by pyroclastic flows from Mount Vesuvius.

Lahars are volcanic mudflows composed of volcanic debris and water. They can travel at speeds of over 60 kilometers per hour and can be highly destructive. The 1985 eruption of Nevado del Ruiz in Colombia generated lahars that buried the town of Armero, killing approximately 23,000 people. Lahars remain a significant hazard around many volcanoes, particularly those with glaciers or heavy rainfall.

Volcanic ash can travel thousands of kilometers from the eruption site. Fine ash particles pose a serious hazard to aircraft engines and can cause engine failure. The 2010 eruption of Eyjafjallajokull in Iceland produced an ash cloud that disrupted air travel across Europe for several weeks, affecting millions of passengers and costing the airline industry billions of dollars.

Volcanic gases, including sulfur dioxide, carbon dioxide, and hydrogen sulfide, can pose hazards both near and far from the eruption site. In 1986, a sudden release of carbon dioxide from Lake Nyos in Cameroon, a volcanic crater lake, killed approximately 1,700 people and 3,500 livestock in nearby villages. The carbon dioxide, being denser than air, flowed down the valleys surrounding the lake, suffocating those in its path.

## Part V: The Science of Earthquakes

An earthquake is the shaking of the surface of the Earth resulting from a sudden release of energy in the Earth's lithosphere that creates seismic waves. Earthquakes can range in size from those that are so weak that they cannot be felt to those violent enough to propel objects and people into the air, and wreak destruction across entire cities.

### Seismic Waves

When an earthquake occurs, it releases energy in the form of seismic waves that radiate outward from the point of rupture, called the focus or hypocenter. The point on the Earth's surface directly above the focus is called the epicenter. Seismic waves are classified into two main types: body waves and surface waves.

Body waves travel through the interior of the Earth. There are two types of body waves: primary waves and secondary waves. Primary waves, also known as P-waves, are compressional waves that cause particles in the rock to move back and forth in the same direction as the wave is traveling. They are the fastest seismic waves and are the first to arrive at a seismograph station. P-waves can travel through solids, liquids, and gases.

Secondary waves, also known as S-waves, are shear waves that cause particles to move perpendicular to the direction of wave travel. S-waves are slower than P-waves and arrive at seismograph stations after the P-waves. Importantly, S-waves cannot travel through liquids, which is how scientists discovered that the Earth's outer core is liquid. The shadow zone for S-waves beyond an angular distance of about 104 degrees from an earthquake provided key evidence for this discovery.

Surface waves travel along the Earth's surface and are generally the most destructive type of seismic wave. There are two types of surface waves: Love waves and Rayleigh waves. Love waves cause horizontal shearing motion perpendicular to the direction of wave propagation. Rayleigh waves cause an elliptical motion of the ground, similar to the motion of ocean waves. Surface waves are slower than body waves but typically have larger amplitudes and cause the most damage during earthquakes.

### Measuring Earthquakes

The Richter magnitude scale, developed by Charles Richter in 1935, was the first widely used method for measuring earthquake magnitude. It is a logarithmic scale, meaning that each whole number increase in magnitude represents a tenfold increase in measured amplitude and approximately 31.6 times more energy release. An earthquake of magnitude 5.0 releases about 31.6 times more energy than a magnitude 4.0 earthquake, and about 1,000 times more energy than a magnitude 3.0 earthquake.

The moment magnitude scale, developed in the 1970s, has largely replaced the Richter scale for measuring large earthquakes. It is based on the seismic moment of the earthquake, which is proportional to the area of the fault that ruptures, the average displacement on the fault, and the rigidity of the rocks. The moment magnitude scale provides a more accurate measure of the total energy released by an earthquake than the Richter scale, especially for very large events.

The Modified Mercalli Intensity scale measures the intensity of shaking at a particular location, rather than the energy released by the earthquake. It uses Roman numerals from I to XII, where I represents shaking that is not felt and XII represents total destruction. The same earthquake can produce different intensities at different locations, depending on distance from the epicenter, local geology, and building construction.

### Historical Earthquakes

The 1906 San Francisco earthquake, estimated at magnitude 7.9, was one of the most significant earthquakes in American history. The earthquake and the fires it triggered destroyed over 80 percent of San Francisco and killed an estimated 3,000 people. The earthquake ruptured the San Andreas Fault for a distance of about 477 kilometers. The disaster led to important advances in earthquake science and building codes.

The 1964 Alaska earthquake, at magnitude 9.2, was the most powerful earthquake recorded in North American history and the second most powerful ever recorded worldwide. The earthquake lasted approximately 4.5 minutes and caused massive landslides and tsunamis. The tsunami generated by the earthquake caused damage as far away as Crescent City, California, where 11 people were killed. The earthquake lifted some areas of land by as much as 11 meters.

The 2004 Indian Ocean earthquake, at magnitude 9.1, triggered a devastating tsunami that killed approximately 230,000 people in 14 countries. It was the third-largest earthquake ever recorded and the deadliest natural disaster of the 21st century. The earthquake ruptured a fault segment approximately 1,300 kilometers long. The resulting tsunami reached heights of up to 30 meters in some areas and traveled across the Indian Ocean at speeds of up to 800 kilometers per hour.

The 2011 Tohoku earthquake in Japan, at magnitude 9.1, generated a massive tsunami that reached heights of up to 40.5 meters. The earthquake and tsunami killed approximately 20,000 people and caused the Fukushima Daiichi nuclear disaster, the worst nuclear accident since Chernobyl. The earthquake shifted the Earth's axis by an estimated 10 to 25 centimeters and shortened the length of the day by 1.8 microseconds.

### Earthquake Prediction and Preparedness

Despite decades of research, reliable short-term prediction of earthquakes remains elusive. Scientists can identify regions where large earthquakes are likely to occur based on the locations of active faults and the history of past earthquakes, but they cannot predict the exact time, location, and magnitude of future earthquakes with useful precision.

Seismic hazard maps identify areas of high earthquake risk based on the probability of ground shaking exceeding certain levels within a given time period. These maps are used to establish building codes, set insurance rates, and plan emergency response. In the United States, the United States Geological Survey produces the National Seismic Hazard Maps, which are updated regularly to incorporate new data and improved models.

Early warning systems can provide seconds to minutes of warning before strong shaking arrives at a location. These systems detect the fast-moving but less destructive P-waves and issue alerts before the slower but more destructive S-waves and surface waves arrive. Japan's earthquake early warning system can provide warnings of up to 30 seconds before strong shaking. The United States ShakeAlert system provides similar capability for the West Coast.

Building codes designed to resist earthquake damage have significantly reduced casualties in developed countries. Modern earthquake-resistant buildings use techniques such as base isolation, in which the building is placed on flexible bearings that absorb seismic energy, and energy dissipation devices that reduce the amplitude of vibrations. These technologies cannot prevent damage entirely, but they can prevent building collapse and save lives.

### Tsunamis

Tsunamis are large ocean waves generated by sudden displacements of the ocean floor, typically caused by earthquakes, volcanic eruptions, or underwater landslides. The word tsunami comes from the Japanese words for harbor and wave. In the open ocean, tsunamis travel at speeds of 500 to 1,000 kilometers per hour with wave heights of less than one meter, making them nearly undetectable. As they approach shallow coastal waters, they slow down and increase dramatically in height.

The physics of tsunami propagation is governed by the shallow water equations. In deep water, the speed of a tsunami is approximately equal to the square root of the product of gravitational acceleration and water depth. In the Pacific Ocean, with an average depth of about 4,000 meters, a tsunami travels at approximately 200 meters per second, or about 720 kilometers per hour. This is comparable to the speed of a commercial jet aircraft.

Tsunami warning systems have been established in the Pacific Ocean since 1949. The Pacific Tsunami Warning Center in Hawaii monitors seismic activity throughout the Pacific and issues warnings when earthquakes of sufficient magnitude occur in locations capable of generating tsunamis. After the 2004 Indian Ocean tsunami, similar warning systems were established for the Indian Ocean, the Caribbean, and the Mediterranean.

The energy of a tsunami is proportional to the square of its wave height and extends through the entire water column, from the surface to the ocean floor. This is fundamentally different from wind-generated waves, which affect only the surface layer of the ocean. A tsunami with a wave height of one meter in deep water can carry as much energy as a wind-generated wave 10 meters high. When this energy is compressed into shallow water near the coast, the results can be catastrophic.

Historical records document tsunamis throughout human history. The eruption of Thera, a volcanic island in the Aegean Sea, around 1600 BCE may have generated a tsunami that contributed to the decline of the Minoan civilization on Crete. The 1755 Lisbon earthquake generated a tsunami that devastated the coast of Portugal, Spain, and North Africa, with wave heights reaching up to 20 meters. The earthquake and tsunami killed an estimated 60,000 to 100,000 people and profoundly influenced European philosophy, inspiring Voltaire's critique of optimism in Candide.

Tsunami preparedness involves education, evacuation planning, and infrastructure. Many coastal communities have established tsunami evacuation routes and vertical evacuation structures. Japan has invested heavily in sea walls and breakwaters, though the 2011 Tohoku tsunami demonstrated that even extensive defenses can be overwhelmed by exceptionally large events. Education remains the most effective tool for reducing tsunami casualties, as people who recognize the warning signs of a tsunami and know how to respond can often reach safety even without formal warning systems.
